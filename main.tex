\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces Blablador, a Large Language Model (LLM) inference server developed by the Helmholtz Foundation and operated at Forschungszentrum Jülich. Blablador is designed to meet the unique needs of the scientific community, offering privacy-preserving features, API accessibility, and support for custom models. The platform serves over 1,000 researchers annually across the Helmholtz Association, providing access to dozens of state-of-the-art models across 66 GPUs. In this article, we discuss its architecture, capabilities, and potential impact on AI research for all scientific domains. Our contributions include: (1) a privacy-first inference infrastructure that does not log user interactions, (2) an OpenAI-compatible API enabling seamless integration with existing research code, (3) support for custom domain-specific models, and (4) a cost-effective alternative to commercial LLM APIs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as transformative tools in the landscape of artificial intelligence and scientific research. These models, exemplified by GPT-3 \cite{brockman2020openai}, PaLM \cite{JMLR:v24:22-1144}, and BERT \cite{devlin2018bert}, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, leading to their adoption across diverse scientific disciplines. 

LLMs have found applications in various domains, including:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Bioinformatics:} Protein structure prediction \cite{jumper2021highly} and genomic sequence analysis \cite{ji2021dnabert}.
    \item \textbf{Chemistry:} Molecular property prediction and drug discovery \cite{vatansever2021artificial}.
    \item \textbf{Climate Science:} Climate model interpretation and extreme event prediction \cite{kochkov2024neural}.
    \item \textbf{Astrophysics:} Celestial object classification and anomaly detection \cite{10578402}.
\end{itemize}

Albeit equipped with astonishing capabilities, LLMs have been found to expose detrimental performance in reasoning tasks \cite{nezhurina2024alicewonderlandsimpletasks}. Moreover, severe doubts have been raised that their cognitive abilities emergent after training could be only due to the way humans interact with them \cite{lu2024emergentabilitieslargelanguage}.
%PS we could continue this enumeration

In this tension between opportunities and failures, the scientific community faces several challenges in effectively leveraging these models:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Limited Access:} State-of-the-art LLMs are often proprietary or require significant computational resources, limiting their accessibility to many researchers.
    
    \item \textbf{Privacy Concerns:} Many existing LLM platforms log user interactions, raising concerns about data confidentiality, especially when working with sensitive or unpublished research.
    
    \item \textbf{Computational Constraints:} Running large-scale LLMs requires substantial computational power, which may not be available to all research institutions.
    
    \item \textbf{Integration Difficulties:} Incorporating LLMs into existing research workflows and software stacks can be technically challenging and time-consuming.
    
    \item \textbf{Model Customization:} Researchers often need to fine-tune or adapt models for domain-specific tasks, which is not always supported by general-purpose LLM platforms.
\end{enumerate}

To address these challenges, the Helmholtz Foundation has developed Blablador, an LLM inference server specifically designed for scientific research. 

\section{Motivation}

Blablador is accessible at \url{https://blablador.fz-juelich.de} to ensure democratic access by researchers across a variety of institutions and scientific domains. The system offers a unique set of features tailored to the needs of the research community:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Privacy-First Approach:} Blablador does not record or store any user conversations or data on the minimalist interface, ensuring the confidentiality of research queries and results. Newer UIs store data only if conversations are not marked as temporary.
    
    \item \textbf{API Accessibility:} A robust API compatible with OpenAI's ecosystem allows seamless integration with existing research code and workflows, facilitating programmatic access to LLM capabilities. API usage has grown 30x since December 2025.
    
    \item \textbf{Multi-Infrastructure Deployment:} Blablador operates across 66 GPUs on three different infrastructures:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Haicluster:} Since 2022, 3 bare-metal nodes with 16x RTX 3090 24GB, managed by Slurm + Easybuild + vLLM. Stable, but given the age, it's being phased out.
        \item \textbf{Jureca-DC West AI:} 4 nodes with 4x H100 80GB each, used primarily for experiments with giant models. Production has been phased out here, as a traditional supercomputer is not well-suited for LLM inference, but it's still available for experiments with large models.
        \item \textbf{JSC Cloud:} Kubernetes-based infrastructure with A100, RTX A6000, V100, L40s, and H100 GPUs. All relevant models run here, steadily improving in stability.
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Model Diversity:} A mix of small, fast models and large, slower ones. Models are selected based on benchmark performance (LM Arena, etc.), community feedback, licensing considerations, and cost-quality tradeoffs. New models are constantly evaluated and added.
    
    \item \textbf{Agentic Workflows:} Blablador serves as the LLM backend for coding agents (OpenClaw, OpenCode, n8n, Continue.dev, and user-defined code) via API. This has driven significant API usage growth and positions Blablador at the center of the agentic coding revolution.
    
    \item \textbf{Open Source Heritage:} Originally forked from FastChat (LM-Sys) in 2022, Blablador today is maintained by a small team with active contributions back to the FastChat project and integration with Synapse, the Helmholtz AI router.
    
    \item \textbf{Team:} Blablador is developed and maintained by a small team of 2 persons - Alexandre Strube and Konstantin Z. Rushchanskii, with hiring in progress.
\end{itemize}

New web interfaces based on Open-WebUI and Librechat are in development, featuring RAG, file uploads, and multi-modal model support.

In the following sections, we present the technical details of Blablador's implementation, explore its research applications, and discuss its implications for open science and collaborative AI development and benchmarking. Through this work, we demonstrate how targeted infrastructure development can address critical needs in the scientific community and pave the way for more accessible, privacy-preserving AI tools in research.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Synapse Router:} A Go-based high-performance router developed by NISL and Tsinghua University \cite{synapse} with contributions from Helmholtz AI. It handles authentication, rate limiting, and efficient load balancing across model instances.
    \item \textbf{vLLM Model Serving:} High-performance LLM inference engine \cite{vllm} for efficient model serving with PagedAttention for memory optimization. PagedAttention reduces memory fragmentation by up to 70\% compared to traditional attention mechanisms, enabling 2-4x higher throughput \cite{vllm}. Blablador uses vLLM as its primary serving engine, comparable to HuggingFace's Text Generation Inference (TGI) \cite{tgi}.
    \item \textbf{Kubernetes Infrastructure:} Cloud-native deployment on JSC Cloud for scalable model serving.
    \item \textbf{Slurm-Based Clusters:} Bare-metal clusters for specialized workloads.
    \item \textbf{Multi-Model Support:} Currently serving 15+ models including, Minimax, Qwen, and domain-specific models like Cosmosage \cite{dehaan2024cosmosage}.
    \item \textbf{Web Interfaces:} Multiple UI options including the classic interface, Librechat-based UI, and Open-WebUI integration.
\end{itemize}

The system now operates across \textbf{66 GPUs across 3 different infrastructures}, providing a mix of small/fast models and large/slow ones to stay current with state-of-the-art LLM development.

\subsection{Related Work}

Several commercial and open-source platforms provide LLM inference services. Blablador differentiates itself through its focus on the scientific community and privacy-first design.

\textbf{Commercial Cloud Services:} Major cloud providers offer LLM inference through services like Amazon SageMaker, Google Vertex AI, and Microsoft Azure OpenAI Service. While these platforms provide high availability and extensive model catalogs, they typically log user interactions for improvement purposes and may not meet the strict privacy requirements of scientific research institutions.

\textbf{Hugging Face Inference Endpoints:} Hugging Face provides managed inference endpoints for thousands of models. However, these run on Hugging Face infrastructure, raising data sovereignty concerns for researchers working with sensitive or unpublished data.

\textbf{Open-Source Solutions:} Platforms like Text Generation Inference (TGI) by Hugging Face and LMdeploy by Mistral AI offer self-hosted inference solutions. These require significant technical expertise to deploy and maintain, which may be a barrier for research teams without dedicated infrastructure support.

\textbf{Blablador's Unique Position:} Unlike these alternatives, Blablador is specifically designed for the scientific community with the following distinguishing features:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Privacy-First:} No logging of user interactions by default, critical for unpublished research
    \item \textbf{Institutional Backing:} Operated by Helmholtz Association, a major European research organization
    \item \textbf{API Compatibility:} OpenAI-compatible API enables easy integration with existing research code
    \item \textbf{Custom Model Support:} Researchers can request hosting of domain-specific models
    \item \textbf{Multi-Infrastructure:} Flexible deployment across different GPU clusters for various use cases
\end{itemize}

This combination of privacy, accessibility, and scientific focus makes Blablador a unique resource for the research community.

\section{Key Features}
\subsection{Privacy-Preserving Design}
While the system uses the Helmholtz authentication and authorization infrastructure to prevent bots and potentially bad actors, usernames themselves are never collected or logged. Additionally, interactions with the models are also not recorded.
This is important for users with unpublished research, or not-yet-granted patents, for example.

For the API Endpoint server, an API Token must be generated at the Helmholtz Codebase's Gitlab Server\cite{helmholtzcodebase}. This ensures that only authorized users are able to access Blablador via API.

\subsection{API Endpoint Server}
Blablador provides a FastApi-based \cite{FastAPI} API, compatible with the one from OpenAI, so text-based codes using the OpenAI API can, for the most part, point the endpoint to Blablador's server, and use Blablador's resources instead of that from OpenAI. The main difference is that one should use the \emph{apiKey} with the API Token from Helmholtz Cloud.

\subsection{Benefits for Programming and Research with LLMs}
Blablador provides two primary benefits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{As a tool:} Users can leverage Blablador for tasks such as question answering, text summarization, translation, and other natural language processing tasks.
    \item \textbf{As a research platform:} Researchers can study LLMs' replies, reasoning patterns, and behavioral characteristics to advance scientific understanding of large language models.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Custom Model Support}
There are two primary ways to run models on Blablador:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Helmholtz Association researchers can request administrators to keep their models running long-term, subject to GPU availability.
    \item Researchers can connect their own inference server to Blablador through the Synapse router. A standardized process for any Helmholtz scientist to plug their own inference instance is in development, using the same authentication system as the API with additional security measures.
\end{itemize}

An example of a model produced by a scientist and running permanently on Blablador is \emph{Cosmosage} \cite{dehaan2024cosmosage}. While not directly from the Helmholtz Association, this model was chosen as an example of a well-maintained model for scientific purposes. It has been constantly improved and is currently on its third incarnation on Blablador. The Cosmosage developer uses Blablador's inference capabilities for long-term evaluation, demonstrating the platform's research value.

\subsection{Reliability and Challenges}
Maintaining a reliable LLM inference platform is a \emph{Sisyphean task}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{New models, new bugs:} Python dependency hell is a constant challenge
    \item \textbf{New features, new bugs:} Every addition introduces potential failure points
    \item \textbf{New infrastructure, new bugs:} Multi-infrastructure deployment multiplies complexity
    
    \item \textbf{Multiple points of failure:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item VM hosting the web server
        \item Kubernetes clusters
        \item OpenStack infrastructure
        \item Hardware failures
        \item Software bugs in dependencies
        \item Supercomputer outages
        \item Maintenance windows that don't always go as planned
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

Despite these challenges, the Haicluster bare-metal deployment has proven remarkably stable, while the JSC Cloud Kubernetes infrastructure continues to improve in reliability. To ensure service quality, Blablador employs an automated watchdog system \cite{blablador-watchdog} that continuously monitors all hosted models by issuing test queries and verifying responses. This system enables rapid detection of model failures or performance degradation.

The watchdog system tracks four key metrics across all hosted models, as visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:watchdog_metrics}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Response Time:} Measures elapsed time from request to complete response, enabling detection of slow or hung models.
    \item \textbf{Throughput (Tokens/sec):} Tracks generation speed to identify performance degradation over time.
    \item \textbf{Success Rate:} Calculates the percentage of successful queries per model, highlighting unreliable deployments.
    \item \textbf{Average Response Time:} Provides baseline performance metrics for capacity planning.
\end{itemize}
By continuously monitoring these metrics, the operations team can proactively address issues before they impact users, maintaining the high availability required for scientific research workflows.

\section{Performance and Scalability}

Blablador's performance characteristics are shaped by its multi-infrastructure design and the diverse range of models it serves. Below we present key performance metrics and scalability observations.

\subsection{Infrastructure Capacity}

Blablador currently operates across three distinct infrastructures with a combined total of 66 GPUs:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Haicluster (Phase-out):} 3 bare-metal nodes with 16x NVIDIA RTX 3090 24GB each (48 total GPUs). Primarily used for smaller models and testing.
    \item \textbf{Jureca-DC West AI (Experimental):} 4 nodes with 4x NVIDIA H100 80GB each (16 total GPUs). Reserved for experiments with large models (300B+ parameters).
    \item \textbf{JSC Cloud (Production):} Mix of NVIDIA A100, RTX A6000, V100, L40s, and H100 GPUs. Serves all production models and handles the majority of user requests.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Model Serving Performance}

Blablador utilizes vLLM for high-throughput model inference, leveraging PagedAttention for memory optimization \cite{vllm}. Typical performance metrics include:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Throughput:} Up to 15,000 tokens/second aggregate across all cloud models (measured via Kubernetes metrics, see Figure~\ref{fig:metrics})
    \item \textbf{Latency:} Average first-token latency of 50-200ms depending on model size and batch size
    \item \textbf{Concurrent Users:} System supports 400+ concurrent API users with dynamic scaling
    \item \textbf{Availability:} 99.5\% uptime for production infrastructure (JSC Cloud)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scalability}

The Kubernetes-based deployment in JSC Cloud enables horizontal scaling:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Automatic Scaling:} Pods scale based on GPU utilization and request queue depth
    \item \textbf{Multi-Cluster Routing:} Synapse router distributes load across clusters based on availability and model requirements
    \item \textbf{Future Capacity:} JARVIS project (JUPITER Advanced Research Vehicle for Inference Services) will add a specialized inference module to JUPITER, funded with approximately €55M via the EuroHPC Joint Undertaking, German Federal Ministry of Education and Research, and state ministries \cite{jarvis}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations}

While Blablador provides significant benefits, we acknowledge several limitations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Library Maintenance Overhead:} The rapidly evolving LLM ecosystem requires continuous library updates. New model releases often require corresponding updates to inference frameworks, creating an ongoing maintenance burden \cite{vllm}.
    \item \textbf{Resource Constraints:} Despite 66 GPUs, demand can exceed supply during peak usage periods. Large models (70B+ parameters) require specialized hardware (H100 GPUs) that are limited.
    \item \textbf{Benchmark Coverage:} While we track usage metrics, comprehensive benchmark evaluation across all hosted models remains challenging \cite{llmbenchmarks}. Model selection is based on community demand and licensing considerations rather than exhaustive benchmarking.
    \item \textbf{Geographic Limitation:} Currently optimized for Helmholtz Association researchers, potentially limiting accessibility for broader scientific community.
    \item \textbf{No Built-in Evaluation:} The platform focuses on inference rather than model evaluation; researchers must conduct their own benchmarking \cite{llmbenchmarks}.
\end{itemize}

    \item Removing models can disrupt users who depend on them for research/work
    \item Transparency about what's available and why is essential
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
Blablador has grown from a simple FastChat fork into a comprehensive LLM inference platform serving the Helmholtz Association and beyond. Key points:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Current State:} Serving approximately 20 models across 3 infrastructures with 66 total GPUs (Haicluster bare-metal, Jureca-DC for experiments, JSC Cloud for production).
    
    \item \textbf{Agentic Revolution:} API usage has grown 30x since December 2025 as Blablador becomes the backbone for coding agents and AI-powered development.
    
    \item \textbf{Near Future:} Two major funding initiatives are underway—Helmholtz Blablador NG project (3.6M €) and JARVIS (50M € total investment)—both planned for Q4 2026 to Q1 2027 deployment.
    
    \item \textbf{Philosophy:} Blablador democratizes access to state-of-the-art AI, enabling researchers to become builders in the age of agentic workflows. We're not just serving models; we're empowering the next generation of AI-powered scientific discovery.
\end{itemize}

As the field evolves, Blablador remains committed to privacy-first design, responsible AI practices, and providing accessible infrastructure for scientific research. The platform sits at the intersection of technical infrastructure, community building, and the broader paradigm shift in how code and research are produced in the age of AI.
